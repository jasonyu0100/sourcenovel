# Arc 1: The Last Night

## Premise
The arc opens in the present day — Idris and Sera together on the Ninth Terrace as a new political crisis threatens to shatter the station's neutrality — then plunges into a flashback to the night humanity forgot about death. The Forgetting: the moment technology made mortality optional, and civilization discovered it had been carrying two incompatible answers. The present-day prologue (Chapter 1) shows who they are now: a retired shopkeeper and a Vice-Commandant, still orbiting each other, still unresolved. The flashback (Chapters 2-6) shows who they were: young, in love, and about to be torn apart by humanity's oldest question. The arc shows what they had, what they lost, and why the question itself made bending impossible.

## Goals
- Establish the present-day stakes before rewinding to the origin
- Show the Idris-Sera relationship at its peak so readers feel the weight of its loss
- Show both characters as they were before grief and distance changed them
- Ground the Forgetting in personal stakes — the split isn't abstract philosophy, it's two people in a room who discovered they'd always been different
- Reveal the worldbuilding through Sera's journey to the Sovereignty — the Forgetting explained through dialogue and observation
- Create the emotional foundation for the entire series

## Structure

### Prologue (Chapter 1) — Present Day
Idris and Sera walk the Grand Promenade after an extraction mission. Their easy intimacy is shadowed by news of formal dissolution — the Senatorial Council has voted, both Thrones are recalling Adepts, envoys arrive tomorrow. The chapter establishes the present-day relationship (intimate but unresolved), the Terrace as neutral ground under threat, and Idris's refusal to engage with the wider conflict. Ends on a held breath — two priority military dispatches they choose not to read.

### Flashback (Chapters 2-6) — The Night of the Forgetting
Years earlier. Idris and Sera — younger, more open, still believing they can have both love and conviction — spend their final night together as civilization chooses whether to remember death or finally move past it. The flashback ends not with the argument, but with Sera's journey to the Sovereignty, where the worldbuilding unfolds through her eyes.

## Setting (Flashback)
The night of the Forgetting. Officers' quarters in the still-unified military, before the Ascendancy and Sovereignty formally split. The outside world is fracturing over humanity's oldest question; inside, two people are discovering they'd always been different. The final chapter follows Sera aboard a military transport as she travels to the Sovereignty — the scale of interstellar civilization revealed, the ideology of the Forgetting externalized through dialogue.

## Characters (Flashback)

### Idris Vael (younger)
Not yet the quiet shopkeeper of the Terrace. This is Idris at his most vital — still active, still engaged with the world, still believing he can have both his principles and the woman he loves. Sharp, commanding, capable of warmth that the present-day Idris has buried. His Discipline is known but not yet legendary. He is someone who still fights for things.

### Sera Kaine (younger)
Not yet the Vice-Commandant. This is Sera before fury became her engine — she's brilliant and ambitious, but also open in a way she won't be later. She laughs. She reaches for him. She hasn't yet learned to weaponize every part of herself. When she chooses the Sovereignty, it's not calculation — it's rage and heartbreak channeled into forward motion.

## Beats (Flashback)

### 1. Together (Chapter 2)
Idris and Sera in a quiet moment before the storm. They're in their shared space — the routines, the shorthand, the easy intimacy of people who fit. The crisis is background noise they've been ignoring. We see the relationship at its peak: the way they complement each other, the small gestures, the silence that isn't empty. Hints of tension — a broadcast they mute, a message left unread.

### 2. The Fracture (Chapter 3)
The crisis becomes impossible to ignore. Orders arrive. Communication channels activate. The unified government is dissolving — not over territory, but over the Forgetting. Technology has made death optional, and humanity must choose. The abstract becomes concrete: Idris and Sera are being called to opposite sides. Not because of origin, but because of how they answered humanity's oldest question.

### 3. Opposite Sides (Chapter 4)
They talk. Then they argue. Not shouting — these are two people who fight with precision and restraint, which makes it worse. Every word lands exactly where it's aimed. Idris believes mortality gives life meaning — the frame of finitude makes choices matter. Sera believes mortality was the cage — every human value shaped by scarcity and fear. Neither is entirely wrong. Neither will bend. The argument isn't about philosophy anymore — it's about whether love is enough to override a fundamental difference in how they want to exist.

### 4. The Walk (Chapter 5)
One of them leaves. The silence after. The empty space where someone was. The door closes quiet and final. The choice is made. The love is still there. It has nowhere to live.

### 5. The Transit (Chapter 6)
Sera aboard a Sovereignty military transport, traveling toward the future she chose. The worldbuilding unfolds here — the scale of interstellar civilization, the ideology of both Thrones, the Forgetting explained through a conversation with Commandant Reva Orin. The open, laughing Sera from the earlier chapters begins becoming the armored Vice-Commandant from the prologue. The arc ends with Vantage filling the viewport — the future, rendered in metal and light — and Sera choosing not to look back.

## Arc Identity
A love story told in two timeframes — the reader meets these people as they are, then watches who they were. The present-day prologue transforms the flashback from backstory into dramatic irony: every tender moment carries the weight of knowing exactly how it ends. The flashback begins claustrophobic — one room, one night, two people — then expands in Chapter 6 as Sera travels to the Sovereignty. The worldbuilding reveals what the Forgetting means: not a political disagreement, but humanity dividing over whether to remain mortal. The arc shows that Idris and Sera didn't choose different sides — they discovered they'd always been different. That's why neither could bend.
