# Shared Quarters

She straightened his collar without thinking about it — her fingers finding the Ascendancy insignia at his throat, adjusting the fold where the extraction had creased it. The metal was still warm from his skin. She smoothed the fabric flat, felt the weight of the rank seal beneath her thumb, and let her hand drop.

Idris caught it on the way down. Pressed his lips to her knuckles, once, quick, and released her.

"Debrief can wait," he said.

"Debrief cannot wait." But she was already smiling.

Their quarters were a small argument against the idea that two Adepts with active commissions could maintain separate lives. His tactical projections still glowed on her desk — the resonance cascade mapped in pale blue light, annotations in his precise handwriting layered over her spatial corridor calculations. Her jacket hung on the back of his chair. A stack of his intelligence briefings had migrated to her side of the bed sometime last week and neither of them had corrected it.

The room was warm. It was always warm — amber lighting that softened the hard angles of military furniture, the low hum of climate systems behind the walls, and beyond the window, the capital's skyline in its usual configuration of spires and transit lines and the distant glow of the Senate complex. Sera had lived in officer housing on three different installations. This was the first one that felt like an address instead of an assignment.

Idris moved through the space the way he moved through everything — with an ease that made people forget how precisely he was paying attention. He poured two glasses of something amber, pulled up the debrief notes on the wallscreen, and handed her a datapad with the display already angled for her grip. He'd been doing that for two years. She'd never asked him to.

"Your cascade timing was off," she said, settling onto the edge of the desk.

"My cascade timing saved the operation."

"By three seconds. The extraction manual recommends twelve."

"The extraction manual was written by people who've never collapsed a resonance cascade." He leaned against the wall, glass in hand, and the warmth in his voice took the edge off the words. This was how they debriefed: her precision against his instinct, her spatial modeling against whatever it was his Discipline did when he reached into the structure of things and pulled. They'd run the extraction today like a single organism — his mapping, her corridors, the team moving through gaps that existed only because two people could think in the same direction at the same time.

Everyone who'd worked with them knew it. Command knew it. The classified synergy reports that neither of them was supposed to have read made it clinical: *Vael-Kaine resonance compatibility, highest recorded bilateral output in current active roster.* As if you could reduce the way they fought together to a measurement.

"Torren filed his transfer orders," Sera said.

Idris glanced up from the debrief notes. "When?"

"Last week. Sovereignty intake processing." She turned the datapad in her hands. "His rotation shifts got redistributed across the unit. Maren picked up his Thursday."

"Hm." Idris took a drink. "People make choices."

She watched him for a moment — the way he dismissed it, clean and unbothered, the way he turned back to the notes as if a colleague choosing a side before there were officially sides to choose was just weather. And maybe it was. Maybe Torren's empty desk in the briefing room and the careful way no one mentioned his name in the corridors was just what happened when a political crisis moved slowly enough that people could walk away from it one at a time instead of all at once.

Through the window, the capital looked the same as it always did. Transit lines pulsing. The Senate dome lit from within. But there were more military convoys on the lower ring-road than usual, and the broadcast tower on the eastern ridge was cycling through advisory frequencies she recognized from field deployments. Sera registered it the way she registered most things — noted, catalogued, not yet requiring action.

Idris set down his glass and crossed the room to where she sat on the desk. He didn't ask. His arm settled around her waist, his hand finding the curve of her hip, and she leaned into him — shoulder against his chest, her head tipped back against his collarbone. The datapad went dark in her lap.

This was the part that didn't fit into synergy reports. The way his thumb traced a slow line along her waist. The way her breathing slowed to match his without either of them deciding to do it. The ease of two people who had learned each other's bodies the same way they'd learned each other's Disciplines — through repetition, attention, and the particular kind of trust that came from knowing someone could destroy you and chose not to.

She could feel his heartbeat through the fabric of his uniform. Steady. Present.

"You're thinking about something," he said.

She was. Torren's transfer. The dissolution vote the Senate kept postponing. The word that had started appearing in classified briefings — *origin* — as if where you were born was a loyalty test you'd never agreed to take. Sera held a Sovereignty commission. Idris held an Ascendancy one. It had never mattered because the government was one thing, and they served one chain of command, and the quarters they shared didn't belong to a Throne — they belonged to a unit.

But units were made of people, and people were choosing, and the choosing was getting closer.

She looked at Idris. His eyes were on the wallscreen — the cascade map still glowing, his annotations sharp against the blue — but his hand was warm on her waist, and his pulse was steady, and he was here. In this room. In this version of the world where a collar bore an insignia and the insignia still meant something and Sera could lean into him without calculating what it cost.

"No," she said. "Nothing important."

The light stayed warm. His hand stayed where it was. Outside, the convoys moved along the lower ring-road in quiet formation, and the broadcast tower cycled through its frequencies, and somewhere in an intake office a colleague's name was being entered into a system that hadn't existed six months ago.

Sera closed her eyes and let the evening be what it was.
