# Chapter 1: Two Kinds of Light

The Ninth Terrace existed seven times faster than the rest of human civilization, and it showed.

The Grand Promenade stretched ahead of them in a long golden curve along the outermost tier — lined with luminara trees that had no business existing this far from any natural sun. Their silver-white bark caught the ambient glow of the Terrace's artificial sky. Their canopy filtered the light into something warm and honeyed, translucent amber leaves meeting overhead in cathedral arches that broke and reformed as the avenue wound toward the viewing plaza. Beyond the low balustrade to their left, the void. Stars. The slow rotation of station-lights in the distance.

No one built anything this beautiful in real-time anymore. The Terrace's dilation — one day outside, roughly a week within — meant artisans could spend a month perfecting a single balustrade when the rest of the galaxy measured that same span in hours. The result was a place that felt ancient and lived-in despite being, in objective time, barely a generation old. Every stone was considered. Every sightline composed. The Promenade existed because someone with a Tier 7 Discipline and two decades of subjective time had decided the outermost tier deserved cathedral arches made of living light.

Sera walked half a step ahead of him. She always did — not rank, not impatience, just the way she moved through the world. Forward. Certain. Her hair was down, loose brunette waves catching the golden light, and she'd changed into something softer — a fitted dark dress with tech-line accents at collar and cuffs. She looked like the avenue had been built for her.

A couple on a bench beneath the nearest luminara rose as they passed — the woman catching Sera's eye, dipping her head in the particular way people did when they recognized an Adept of significant rank but weren't sure whether to acknowledge it. Sera returned the nod without breaking stride. Idris, a step behind in his mandarin collar suit still creased from the extraction, received no such recognition. That was the point.

He felt the Promenade in the way he felt everything — the faint harmonic of the dilation field beneath his feet, the ambient resonance signatures of every Adept within two hundred meters. Fourteen, at this hour. Three above Tier 5, including the groundskeeper tending the luminara canopy with delicate pulses of biokinetic energy. The sensation was automatic, constant, and something he had never told anyone about except Sera. His Discipline didn't turn off. It catalogued the world whether he wanted it to or not.

"The Senatorial Council voted," Sera said, not looking back. Her voice carried easily in the still air. "Formal dissolution. Two separate governing bodies by the end of the standard week."

Idris said nothing. The leaves drifted around them like slow amber snow.

"Fleet Command has already split the order of battle," she continued. "Every active-duty Adept with a commission is being recalled to their Throne of origin."

"I know."

She glanced over her shoulder. The light caught the line of her jaw, the curve of her mouth — and the edge beneath it. "You always know. You just never do anything about it."

They walked in silence for a stretch. The avenue opened into the viewing plaza — a semicircle of golden stone suspended over the void, the Terrace's tiered architecture rising behind them in illuminated layers. It was the kind of view that made visiting diplomats from both Thrones stop and reconsider their loyalties. The Terrace didn't belong to the Ascendancy. It didn't belong to the Sovereignty. It belonged to itself, and everything it built was a quiet argument for why that should continue.

Sera stopped at the balustrade and leaned against it, facing him. Stars behind her. Golden light behind him. She tilted her head — evaluating, fond, sharp all at once.

"You've read your recall orders."

"I haven't opened them."

"Idris."

"I haven't opened them because there's nothing in them I need to read."

The word hung between them unspoken. *Origin.* Idris was born in Ascendancy space. Trained in Ascendancy academies. His commission — the one he still technically held, buried under years of inactive status and Terrace residency — bore an Ascendancy seal. Sera's bore a Sovereignty one.

He could feel her heartbeat from here. Sixty-two beats per minute. Steady. Controlled. Sera's heart rate never betrayed her unless she wanted it to. But her fingers, resting on the stone between them — those were pressed flat. Anchoring herself.

"The Terrace Council issued a neutrality reaffirmation this morning," Idris said. "Sovereign territory, independent jurisdiction. Neither Throne has authority here."

"The Terrace Council can reaffirm whatever it likes. That holds exactly as long as both Thrones decide to respect it." She studied him. The light from the luminara canopy shifted across her face — warm gold, then cool where the starlight crept in from beyond the balustrade. Two kinds of light on one face. He thought that was about right.

"They'll send someone," she said. "Both Thrones will. Not just recall notices — actual envoys. They'll come here, to the Terrace, and they'll want to know where the most powerful Adepts stand. Including you."

"I'm a retired shopkeeper."

"You're a retired shopkeeper who collapsed a resonance cascade today with three seconds less margin than the most elite extraction team in either Throne could manage." Her voice softened. Not in volume — in the thing underneath it. "You can't hide in this place forever."

"It's not hiding. It's choosing."

"It's the same thing, and you know it."

A transport slid past in the middle distance, its running lights blinking against the dark. A small bright thing moving through an enormous emptiness.

"Come here," she said, quieter now.

He turned to face her. Sera reached up and straightened his collar — a small, automatic gesture, her fingers adjusting the mandarin fold where it had creased. She did it without thinking about it. The way you fix something that belongs to you.

Idris caught her hand. Held it flat against his chest, over the steady beat beneath his sternum. She looked up at him, and for a moment neither of them was performing anything. Not composure, not control, not the careful architecture of two people who spent their professional lives being watched and measured. Just this. The golden light. The quiet after a mission that could have killed them. The most beautiful avenue in any world, and the only person in it who mattered.

Her fingers curled against his shirt.

He leaned down and pressed his lips to her forehead. She closed her eyes.

Somewhere behind them, two notification tones chimed in unison from their personal channels — the distinctive double-pulse of a priority military dispatch, cutting through the Promenade's quiet like a held breath breaking. Idris felt Sera's hand tense against his chest. He didn't look. She didn't either.

The golden light held. The luminara leaves drifted around them like slow amber snow. Tomorrow, the first envoys from both Thrones would arrive at the Ninth Terrace, and the careful neutrality of the only place in human space where Idris and Sera could exist in the same sentence would begin to crack.

But that was tomorrow.

Tonight, her hand was warm against his chest, and Idris chose not to count the seconds.
