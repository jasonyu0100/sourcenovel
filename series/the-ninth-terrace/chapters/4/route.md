# Chapter 4 Route: Opposite Sides

Sera breaks the silence. She looks at the datapad face-down on the table between them and asks if he's ever going to open it. Her voice is level — not accusation, not yet. Just the question he's been refusing to answer. Idris feels her heartbeat steady at sixty-four, the same controlled rhythm she uses before difficult briefings.

Idris says he knows what it says. The words don't need reading. Sera's fingers press flat against the table — the anchoring gesture he's seen before impossible operations. She asks what he's waiting for, then. Permission? An exception? The political fracture to politely reroute around their quarters?

He defends the Terrace option — neutral ground, sovereign jurisdiction. Neither Throne has authority here. They could both stay. Sera's laugh is short, sharp. She asks if he really believes a line on a treaty will protect them when both sides decide it won't. He sounds like someone hiding, not someone choosing.

Sera pushes harder. The Ascendancy is recalling by birth registry — not request, demand. His origin is in their files. Every synergy report, every mission, every reason they want him. And they don't just want him — they want what he and Sera produce together. She's in those files too.

Idris counters: walking away doesn't fix the system. It just hands victory to the people who want it broken. The Ascendancy has rot, but it also has institutions, stability, people who depend on it holding. Abandoning them isn't principle — it's cowardice dressed as conviction.

Sera goes still. She asks if that's what he thinks she's doing. Running. The Sovereignty isn't running — it's building something new because the old thing is dying and he refuses to see it. Staying inside a collapsing structure isn't loyalty. It's denial.

They're closer now — the three meters have become two. Idris can feel her pulse climbing for the first time. She's not controlling it anymore. Neither is he. The light in the room has shifted — the amber warmth bleeding toward the blue-white of the screens behind them.

Sera says what she's been holding: "If you loved me enough, you'd come with me." The words land exactly where she aimed them. Idris feels them hit. He doesn't flinch. He gives her the same precision back: "If you loved me enough, you wouldn't ask me to abandon what I believe."

The silence that follows is worse than the argument. They're standing close enough to touch and neither of them reaches. Sera's heartbeat is at eighty-two now. His is higher. The room feels smaller than it did an hour ago, the door larger.

Sera's voice when she speaks again is quiet, almost gentle. She says she can't stay. Not because she doesn't love him — because staying means becoming someone who waits while the world burns. She was never going to be that person. She thought he knew that.

Idris doesn't answer. There's nothing to say that wouldn't be a lie or a wound. Sera waits three seconds — he counts them without meaning to — then walks to the door. She doesn't look back. The latch clicks. The door doesn't slam. It just closes, quiet and final, and the room holds its breath.

The quarters are the same. His books on her desk. Her jacket still on his chair. The datapad face-down on the table, never opened. Everything looks exactly like it did an hour ago, except there's one fewer person in it, and the silence has a shape now — the shape of someone who just left.
