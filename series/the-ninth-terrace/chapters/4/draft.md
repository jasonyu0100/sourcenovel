# opposite sides

sera broke the silence.

"You're not going to open it, are you."

It wasn't a question. She was looking at the datapad on the table between them — face-down, exactly where Idris had placed it after Maren left. His recall orders. The words he already knew were waiting inside.

Her heartbeat was steady at sixty-four. The controlled rhythm she used before difficult briefings, before missions that might kill them, before any conversation where the outcome mattered more than comfort. She was preparing for something.

"I know what it says," Idris said. "The words don't need reading."

Sera's fingers pressed flat against the table. He recognized the gesture — the same anchoring she used before impossible operations. The physical cost of not breaking.

"Then what are you waiting for?" Her voice was level, precise. "Permission? An exception? The political fracture to politely reroute around our quarters?"

"The Terrace—"

"Don't." The word landed clean. "Don't tell me about the Terrace. Neutral ground. Sovereign jurisdiction. Neither Throne has authority here." She tilted her head, and the light caught her eyes in a way that made something in his chest tighten. "Do you actually believe a line on a treaty will protect you when both sides decide it won't?"

"It's not about protection. It's about choosing where to stand."

"It's about hiding." No hesitation. No cruelty either — just the flat clarity of someone stating what they saw. "You sound like someone hiding, Idris. Not someone choosing."

He didn't answer. There wasn't an answer that wouldn't prove her point.

Sera pushed off from the table and crossed to the window. The city lights below were wrong — more military convoys than usual, broadcast towers cycling through frequencies that belonged in field deployments, not capitals. The fracture wasn't coming. It was here.

"The Ascendancy is recalling by birth registry," she said. "Not request. Demand. Your origin is in their files. Every synergy report. Every mission. Every reason they want you." She turned to face him. "And they don't just want you. They want what we produce together. I'm in those files too."

"I know."

"Then you know staying neutral isn't an option they're offering."

Idris felt the argument building — the shape of it, the inevitability. They'd been circling this for days without naming it. Now Maren had named it for them, and the room had no more space for silence.

"Walking away doesn't fix the system," he said. "It just hands victory to the people who want it broken. The Ascendancy has rot — I'm not blind to that. But it also has institutions. Stability. People who depend on it holding together." He met her eyes. "Abandoning them isn't principle. It's cowardice dressed as conviction."

Sera went still.

The light in the room had shifted while they talked. The amber warmth bleeding toward the blue-white of the screens behind her. She looked like something carved from the edge between two worlds — which, he supposed, she was.

"Is that what you think I'm doing?" Her voice was quiet. Too quiet. "Running?"

"I think you're angry. I think the Ascendancy failed you in ways I can't fix, and you're channeling that into something that feels like forward motion." He took a breath. "The Sovereignty isn't salvation. It's just newer rot that hasn't had time to show."

"The Sovereignty is building something." She took a step toward him. The three meters between them became two. "The Ascendancy is dying and you refuse to see it. Staying inside a collapsing structure isn't loyalty, Idris. It's denial."

Her heartbeat was climbing. Seventy-two now. Then seventy-six. She wasn't controlling it anymore.

Neither was he.

"Come with me." The words left her like something she'd been holding too long. "Forget the commissions. Forget the files. We walk out of here tonight and we figure out the rest together."

"And abandon everyone I swore to protect?"

"Protect them from what? From a government that's already fractured? From orders that will come whether you read them or not?" She was close now. Close enough to touch. "You can't save an institution by pretending it isn't burning."

Idris looked at her — the woman he'd loved for three years, standing in the quarters they'd built together, asking him to leave everything else behind. He could feel her pulse from here. Eighty-two. The fastest he'd ever known it outside of combat.

"If you loved me enough," she said, "you'd come with me."

The words landed exactly where she'd aimed them.

He felt them hit. The precision of it — the way she knew exactly where the wound would open. This was how they fought. Not with volume. With accuracy.

He gave her the same.

"If you loved me enough, you wouldn't ask me to abandon what I believe."

The silence that followed was worse than the argument.

They stood close enough to touch and neither of them reached. Sera's heartbeat was steady again — she'd forced it down, the way she forced everything down when it mattered too much. Idris could feel his own pulse in his throat, loud in the quiet room.

The door behind her seemed larger than it had an hour ago. Everything else seemed smaller.

"I can't stay." Her voice was gentle now, which made it worse. "Not because I don't love you. Because staying means becoming someone who waits while the world burns." She held his gaze. "I was never going to be that person. I thought you knew that."

He didn't answer. There was nothing to say that wouldn't be a lie or a wound.

Sera waited. Three seconds — he counted them without meaning to. Then she turned and walked to the door.

She didn't look back.

The latch clicked. The door didn't slam. It just closed, quiet and final, and the room held its breath.

Idris stood in the space she'd left behind. His books were still stacked on her desk. Her jacket was still draped over his chair. The datapad was still face-down on the table, never opened.

Everything looked exactly like it had an hour ago.

Except there was one fewer person in it now, and the silence had a shape — the shape of someone who had just walked out, and the door that had closed behind her, and the words that neither of them could take back.

Outside, the convoys kept moving. The broadcasts kept cycling. The fracture kept happening.

And Idris stood alone in the quarters that used to be theirs, and didn't count the seconds, because counting them would make them real.
