# The Interception

The door closed behind Sera with a sound like the end of something.

Idris stood in the center of the room that used to be theirs. Her jacket was still draped over his chair. The datapad lay face-down on the table where he'd placed it hours ago, still unopened. The air held the faint trace of her — something clean and sharp that he'd stopped noticing years ago because it was always there.

He didn't count the seconds since she'd left. Counting would make them real.

Instead he focused on the silence. The hum of the climate system. The distant sound of convoys moving through the capital, military formations splitting along lines that hadn't existed a week ago. The shape of the space where someone used to be.

His Discipline registered the approach before his ears did — a resonance signature he recognized instantly, though he hadn't felt it in years. Familiar. Controlled. Running warm with anticipation rather than fear.

Valeria Caine.

Idris's pulse stayed steady, but something in his chest tightened. He didn't move from where he stood.

The knock came three seconds later. Unhurried. Confident. The door opened without waiting for an answer — she never had waited — and Valeria stepped into the quarters like she belonged there.

Ascendancy dress uniform, immaculate. Dark hair swept back from a face that had aged well, sharper now than it had been when they were young and stupid together. Her smile carried the warmth of someone who already knew she'd won something.

"Idris." She let his name sit in the air. "You look terrible."

"Valeria." He didn't return the smile. "You're supposed to be on Meridian."

"I was." She surveyed the room — the books stacked on the desk that wasn't his, the jacket that wasn't his, the datapad that contained orders he hadn't opened. Her smile didn't change, but something in her eyes sharpened. "The Ascendancy sent me. Official business."

"The Ascendancy doesn't send you for paperwork."

"No." She moved further into the room, her steps unhurried. "They don't."

---

Sera walked through the corridor toward the secondary port. Her pace was measured, deliberate. The lights flickered as another convoy passed overhead — military transport, Sovereignty markings, heading for the transit corridor that would take them out of the capital's jurisdiction.

She didn't look back.

The choice was made. The door was closed. The man she loved was standing in a room that used to be theirs, and she was walking toward a future that didn't include him.

Her fingers pressed flat against her thigh — the anchoring gesture she used when composure cost something. The physical price of not breaking.

---

Valeria closed the distance between them. Not aggressive — just natural, the way she'd always moved around him. Like proximity was assumed. Like the years between then and now were a detail she'd chosen to overlook.

"I heard about your synergy partner," she said. "Kaine, isn't it? Vice-Commandant now. Very impressive." The words carried no admiration. "The Ascendancy doesn't care about personal complications, Idris. They just want you home."

"And they sent you to deliver that message."

"I volunteered." Her smile widened, just slightly. "Old times."

Idris read her heartbeat without meaning to: eighty-four, controlled but elevated. She wasn't afraid. She was anticipating. The same rhythm she'd had years ago, before missions that were personal rather than professional.

"How long were you waiting outside?"

The question landed. Valeria's composure flickered — not guilt, something closer to satisfaction.

"Long enough to hear the argument." She didn't pretend otherwise. "Long enough to wait for the door to close."

She reached for his collar. The gesture Sera had made a thousand times — fingers finding the fabric, adjusting the fold. But Sera's touch had been automatic. Valeria's was deliberate. Her fingers brushed the space where rank insignia used to sit.

"I miss who you were," she said. Her voice dropped, softer now. Intimate. "The man who fought for things. The man who didn't hide on a station at the edge of nowhere while the world burned."

"That's not why I'm here."

"Isn't it?" Her hand stayed on his collar. "You don't have to be the man who stands alone in an empty room, Idris. You don't have to wait for someone who just walked out on you."

---

Sera reached the transport dock.

The secondary port was chaos managed — officers directing traffic, civilians being processed through checkpoints that hadn't existed yesterday, the low hum of engines warming for departure. Maren stood at the boarding ramp, her field pack over one shoulder.

Their eyes met across the platform.

Maren's expression carried relief, question, grief — the complicated weight of someone who'd made a choice and needed to know others had made it too. She raised one hand, a gesture that was half greeting and half plea.

Sera gave her nothing. A nod, brief and professional. The acknowledgment of a colleague, not a friend.

She boarded the transport without looking back.

---

"The Sovereignty will break her," Valeria said. "The way it breaks everyone who thinks ambition is the same as conviction. She'll climb and climb and one day she'll look around and realize she's alone at a height that doesn't mean anything."

"You don't know her."

"I know the type." Valeria moved closer. Her hand stayed on his collar, her body close enough now that he could feel the warmth of her through the uniform. "I know what happens to people who choose causes over people. And I know what you're feeling right now."

The pull wasn't desire. It was exhaustion.

The temptation of someone easier. Someone who didn't ask him to choose between love and conviction. Someone who wanted him without conditions, without complications, without the weight of everything that had just walked out the door.

Valeria had always known how to offer exactly what the wounded part of him wanted.

"Come back with me," she said. "Come home. The Ascendancy will take care of the rest — the paperwork, the politics, all of it. You just have to say yes."

She leaned in. Close enough that he could feel her breath. The almost-kiss hung between them — a question neither of them had answered yet.

Valeria waited. Patient. Certain. She'd done this before. She knew how it ended.

Idris caught her wrist.

Gently, but final. He moved her hand away from his collar, away from the space where Sera's fingers had been, and let go.

"Sera just walked through that door," he said. His voice was quiet. Steady. The voice of someone stating facts rather than making arguments. "And everything that matters walked out with her."

Valeria's composure cracked — not anger, something sadder. The first honest expression he'd seen from her all night.

"You'll regret this," she said. "When she forgets you. When the Sovereignty grinds her into someone you don't recognize. You'll wish you'd taken what was offered."

"Maybe."

"I'll be waiting." She straightened her uniform, smoothing the fabric where her hand had been. "When you change your mind."

She walked to the door and paused at the threshold. For a moment she looked like she might say something else — something real, something that cost her. But Valeria had never been good at vulnerability. She'd always been better at strategy.

The door closed behind her with the same quiet click that had ended everything with Sera.

---

Sera found a seat by the window.

The transport lifted, engines thrumming through the hull, and the capital fell away beneath her. City lights scattered like stars. Military convoys traced lines across the darkness. The world she'd known was splitting in half, and she was choosing her side of the fracture.

She didn't cry. She just watched until there was nothing left to see.

---

Idris stood alone again.

His books on her desk. Her jacket on his chair. The datapad still face-down, still unopened. The room held the shapes of everyone who'd left — Sera's absence, Valeria's perfume, the ghost of who he used to be.

He picked up the datapad.

His recall orders. The words he'd been avoiding all night. He read them once — the official language, the bureaucratic precision, the demand dressed as request — and set the device down again.

The choice was never about the orders. It was about whether he could become someone who waited while the world burned. He couldn't ask Sera to be that person. He couldn't be that person either.

Outside, the convoys kept moving. The fracture kept happening. Somewhere above him, a transport cleared the atmosphere carrying the woman he loved toward a future he wouldn't be part of.

And Idris stood alone in the quarters that used to be theirs, finally counting the seconds, because not counting them didn't make them any less real.
