# Chapter 6 Route: The Transit

Sera feels the FTL transition through her Discipline — space folding around the transport like origami, the universe briefly *wrong* before snapping into a new configuration. Light-years crossed in the time between heartbeats. Somewhere behind them, in a different fold of space-time, Idris is standing alone in quarters that used to be theirs. Her fingers press flat against the viewport. The anchoring gesture. She's done it three times tonight.

The transport is military — Sovereignty construction, sharp angles, everything *built* rather than inherited. No ornamentation. No history in the walls. Just function and forward motion. Officers fill the passenger bay, some in uniform, some still in civilian dress from wherever they were when the Senate dissolved. A woman across the aisle has a child's drawing tucked into her jacket pocket — visible, deliberate. The small details that say: I chose this, and here's what it cost.

Through the viewport: convoy formations spread across the void like scattered stars with purpose. Fleet elements repositioning, their running lights blinking coordination codes Sera can almost read. Dozens of ships visible from this angle alone. Tactical displays show hundreds more. And this is just *one* corridor of one transit route. Two Thrones. Thousands of worlds. Millions of people choosing tonight whether humanity should remember death or finally move past it. The intimacy of what she lost collapses against the vastness of what she's joining.

A woman settles into the seat across from her — older, sharp-featured, wearing Commandant insignia with the ease of someone who's worn it longer than Sera's been commissioned. She doesn't introduce herself. Doesn't make social sounds. She just studies Sera with the flat evaluation of someone who reads synergy reports for a living. Her eyes move to Sera's commission seal, to her posture, to her hands still pressed against the viewport glass.

The Commandant speaks: the Sovereignty doesn't recruit by origin or inheritance. It recruits by conviction. Everyone on this transport chose to forget — which means everyone has something they're leaving behind *on purpose*. She asks what Sera left behind. The question lands like a knife: clean, surgical, testing the wound.

Sera considers deflecting with rank and commission. Considers the careful nothing she'd give anyone else. Instead — because she's raw, because the armor hasn't finished going on — she answers: the other half of the highest bilateral synergy ever recorded. A man who chose to remember. The words come out flat, clinical. Easier that way. Like reading someone else's file.

The Commandant's expression doesn't change, but something in her posture shifts — recognition, or calculation, or both. She introduces herself: Reva Orin. Strategic Command. She's been reading transfer files all night. Sera's was flagged before she even boarded. Not for the synergy — for leaving it. Reva says: you walked away from Paragon-tier output because he wouldn't cross over. That's either conviction or tragedy. She pauses. Probably both.

Reva lays out the choice in clean terms — the way a surgeon explains an incision. The Ascendancy chose to remember death, to hold onto mortality as meaning. They believe finitude is what makes choices matter. That without the frame of ending, existence becomes endless drift. They're not wrong. They're *choosing* limitation the way someone chooses a discipline. The Sovereignty chose differently. To forget. To build past the old limits, past the fear that shaped every human value for a hundred thousand years. Also not wrong. That's what makes it war. That's what makes it matter.

Sera pushes back — she needs to hear the counterargument from someone who rejected it. What about weight? What about consequence? If nothing ends, does anything matter? Reva's response is precise: mortality wasn't meaning. Mortality was the cage. Every value humanity ever held was shaped by scarcity, fear, the knowledge of ending. Remove the cage and you discover what humans *actually* want when they're not terrified. Some people can't handle that freedom. Some people need the frame. Some people — Reva's eyes hold Sera's — choose the weight because existence without it feels like falling.

The conversation shifts — not recruitment anymore, not evaluation. Something closer to recognition. Reva sees what Sera is becoming: someone who chose the future over the person who couldn't follow her there. That's the Sovereignty's founding story. That's the story of everyone on this transport. Reva says: the grief doesn't go away. You just learn to use it as fuel for building something that outlasts it. The words land like permission. Like absolution for a choice Sera hasn't finished making.

Sera feels something shift — the first layer of armor sliding into place. Not numbness. Not denial. A *choice* about what kind of existence she wants. She says: I wasn't going to be someone who waits to die. The sentence hangs there, and she realizes it's true — has always been true, since before she knew the question had words. Reva nods once, like a teacher acknowledging a correct answer. The test is over. Whatever comes next, Sera passed.

The viewport fills with light — Vantage emerging from FTL shadow. An orbital city-station larger than anything Sera has seen, still being built, construction frames reaching into the void like fingers grasping toward forever. Thousands of ships in formation around it. Shipyards and research stations and the visible infrastructure of a Throne that constructs its future rather than inheriting it. The future, rendered in metal and light, and Sera is about to become part of it.

She doesn't look back toward where she came from. She's done looking back. Whatever she was before tonight — the woman who laughed, who reached, who straightened his collar without thinking — that woman made a choice. This woman will live with it. For as long as living means.
