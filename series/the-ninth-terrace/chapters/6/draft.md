# The Transit

Sera felt the FTL transition through her Discipline — space folding around the transport like origami, the universe briefly *wrong* before snapping into a new configuration. Light-years compressed into the space between heartbeats. Somewhere behind them, in a different fold of space-time, Idris was standing alone in quarters that used to be theirs.

Her fingers pressed flat against the viewport. The anchoring gesture. She'd done it three times tonight.

The transport was military — Sovereignty construction, all sharp angles and function. No ornamentation. No history in the walls. Just forward motion made structural. Officers filled the passenger bay, some in uniform, some still in civilian dress from wherever they'd been when the Senate dissolved. A woman across the aisle had a child's drawing tucked into her jacket pocket, the edge visible, deliberate. The small costs of choosing, made visible in paper and ink.

Through the viewport: convoy formations spread across the void like scattered stars with purpose. Dozens of ships in this corridor alone. Tactical displays showed hundreds more. And this was just one transit route of many. Two Thrones. Thousands of worlds. Millions of people choosing tonight whether humanity should remember death or finally move past it.

The intimacy of what she'd lost collapsed against the vastness of what she was joining.

A woman settled into the seat across from her. Older. Sharp-featured. Commandant insignia worn with the ease of long familiarity. She didn't introduce herself or make social sounds — just studied Sera with the flat evaluation of someone who read synergy reports for a living. Her eyes moved to the commission seal, to the posture, to the hands still pressed against the viewport glass.

"The Sovereignty doesn't recruit by origin," the Commandant said. No preamble. "It recruits by conviction. Everyone on this transport chose to forget — which means everyone has something they're leaving behind on purpose." A pause, measured. "What did you leave behind?"

The question landed clean. Surgical. Testing the wound.

Sera considered deflecting with rank and commission. The careful nothing she'd give anyone else. But she was raw, and the armor hadn't finished going on, and the words came out before she could shape them into something safer: "The other half of the highest bilateral synergy ever recorded. A man who chose to remember."

Flat. Clinical. Like reading someone else's file.

The Commandant's expression didn't change, but something in her posture shifted. Recognition. Calculation. Both. "Reva Orin. Strategic Command." She tilted her head slightly. "Your file was flagged before you boarded. Not for the synergy — for leaving it. You walked away from Paragon-tier output because he wouldn't cross over." A beat. "That's either conviction or tragedy."

"Probably both."

"Usually is."

Reva laid out the choice the way a surgeon explains an incision — clean, precise, no comfort offered. The Ascendancy chose to remember death. To hold mortality as meaning. They believed finitude was what made choices matter, that without the frame of ending, existence became endless drift. They weren't wrong. They were *choosing* limitation the way someone chooses a discipline.

The Sovereignty chose differently. To forget. To build past the old limits, past the fear that had shaped every human value for a hundred thousand years.

"Also not wrong," Reva said. "That's what makes it war. That's what makes it matter."

Sera pushed back — she needed to hear the counterargument from someone who'd rejected it. "What about weight? What about consequence? If nothing ends, does anything matter?"

Reva's response was precise: "Mortality wasn't meaning. Mortality was the cage. Every value humanity ever held was shaped by scarcity. By fear. By the knowledge of ending. Remove the cage and you discover what humans *actually* want when they're not terrified." Her eyes held Sera's. "Some people can't handle that freedom. Some people need the frame. Some people choose the weight because existence without it feels like falling."

The words landed somewhere unexpected. Not recruitment. Not evaluation. Something closer to recognition.

"You chose the future over someone who couldn't follow you there," Reva said. "That's the Sovereignty's founding story. That's the story of everyone on this transport." She leaned back slightly. "The grief doesn't go away. You just learn to use it as fuel for building something that outlasts it."

Permission. Absolution. For a choice Sera hadn't finished making.

She felt something shift — the first layer of armor sliding into place. Not numbness. Not denial. A *choice* about what kind of existence she wanted.

"I wasn't going to be someone who waits to die."

The sentence hung there, and she realized it was true. Had always been true, since before she knew the question had words.

Reva nodded once. Like a teacher acknowledging a correct answer. "No. You weren't."

The test was over. Whatever came next, Sera had passed.

The viewport filled with light.

Vantage emerged from FTL shadow — an orbital city-station larger than anything Sera had seen. Construction frames reached into the void like fingers grasping toward forever, still building, still becoming. Thousands of ships in formation around it. Shipyards and research stations and the visible infrastructure of a Throne that constructed its future rather than inheriting it.

The future, rendered in metal and light.

She was about to become part of it.

Sera didn't look back toward where she'd come from. She was done looking back. Whatever she'd been before tonight — the woman who laughed, who reached, who straightened his collar without thinking — that woman had made a choice.

This woman would live with it.

For as long as living meant.
