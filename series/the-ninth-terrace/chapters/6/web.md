# Overview
Hours after walking out on Idris, Sera boards a military transport to Sovereignty space. A conversation with Commandant Reva Orin externalizes the existential divide at the heart of the Forgetting — why some chose to remember death and others to forget it. The transformation from open, laughing Sera to armored Vice-Commandant begins here.
> The worldbuilding chapter. The Forgetting revealed not through exposition but through debate — two sharp minds circling each other, testing positions, recognizing something in each other.

## The Crossing
Sera feels FTL through her Spatial Discipline — the universe folding wrong, then snapping into a new configuration light-years away. Behind her, in a different fold of space-time, Idris stands alone.
> Her Discipline makes the crossing visceral. The distance from Idris becomes physical, measurable in light-years.
"Space folding around the transport like origami, the universe briefly *wrong* before snapping into a new configuration."

### The Anchoring Gesture
Fingers pressed flat against the viewport. She's done it three times tonight.
> The tell that says composure is costing something — the same gesture from chapters 3, 4, and 5.

## The Transport
Military vessel, Sovereignty construction. Sharp angles and function, no ornamentation, no history in the walls. Forward motion made structural.
> The design philosophy of the Sovereignty made physical — everything built rather than inherited.

### The Passengers
Officers in uniform and civilian dress, everyone on board choosing the same thing tonight. A woman across the aisle has a child's drawing tucked into her pocket, visible, deliberate.
> Everyone has something they're leaving behind on purpose. The small costs of choosing, made visible in paper and ink.
"The small costs of choosing, made visible in paper and ink."

### The Scale
Convoy formations spread across the void. Dozens of ships visible, hundreds on tactical displays. This is just one corridor of one transit route.
> The intimacy of what she lost collapses against the vastness of what she's joining.
"Two Thrones. Thousands of worlds. Millions of people choosing tonight whether humanity should remember death or finally move past it."

## Commandant Reva Orin
A woman settles across from Sera — older, sharp-featured, Commandant insignia worn with long familiarity. She studies Sera with the flat evaluation of someone who reads synergy reports for a living.
> Reva as intellectual equal, not recruiter or mentor. The conversation will be two people fencing.

### The Question
"What did you leave behind?" The question lands clean, surgical, testing the wound.
> The Sovereignty recruits by conviction. Everyone on this transport chose to forget — which means everyone has something they're leaving behind on purpose.
"The question landed clean. Surgical. Testing the wound."

### Sera's Answer
The other half of the highest bilateral synergy ever recorded. A man who chose to remember. The words come out flat, clinical — like reading someone else's file.
> She considers deflecting, but she's raw, and the armor hasn't finished going on. The truth emerges before she can shape it into something safer.
"Flat. Clinical. Like reading someone else's file."

### Conviction or Tragedy
Reva's file flagged Sera before she boarded — not for the synergy, but for leaving it. Walking away from Paragon-tier output because he wouldn't cross over.
> "That's either conviction or tragedy." "Probably both." "Usually is."

## The Forgetting Explained
Reva lays out the choice the way a surgeon explains an incision — clean, precise, no comfort offered.
> The ideological divide externalized through debate, not exposition.

### The Ascendancy Position
They chose to remember death. Mortality as meaning — the frame of finitude making choices matter. Without ending, existence becomes endless drift.
> They're not wrong. They're *choosing* limitation the way someone chooses a discipline.
"They believed finitude was what made choices matter, that without the frame of ending, existence became endless drift."

### The Sovereignty Position
They chose to forget. To build past the old limits, past the fear that shaped every human value for a hundred thousand years.
> Also not wrong. That's what makes it war. That's what makes it matter.
"The Sovereignty chose differently. To forget. To build past the old limits."

### Sera Pushes Back
What about weight? What about consequence? If nothing ends, does anything matter?
> She needs to hear the counterargument from someone who rejected it.

### The Cage
Mortality wasn't meaning. Mortality was the cage. Every value shaped by scarcity, fear, the knowledge of ending. Remove the cage and discover what humans actually want when they're not terrified.
> Some people can't handle that freedom. Some people need the frame. Some people choose the weight because existence without it feels like falling.
"Remove the cage and you discover what humans *actually* want when they're not terrified."

## Recognition
The conversation shifts — not recruitment, not evaluation. Something closer to recognition. Reva sees what Sera is becoming.
> "You chose the future over someone who couldn't follow you there. That's the Sovereignty's founding story."
"The grief doesn't go away. You just learn to use it as fuel for building something that outlasts it."

### The Armor Goes On
Sera feels the first layer sliding into place. Not numbness. Not denial. A choice about what kind of existence she wants.
> Permission. Absolution. For a choice she hadn't finished making.

### The Truth
"I wasn't going to be someone who waits to die." The sentence hangs there, and she realizes it's true — has always been true, since before she knew the question had words.
> Reva nods once. Like a teacher acknowledging a correct answer. The test is over.
"Had always been true, since before she knew the question had words."

## Vantage
The viewport fills with light. The Sovereignty's capital emerges from FTL shadow — an orbital city-station larger than anything Sera has seen, still being built, construction frames reaching into the void.
> The future, rendered in metal and light. A Throne that constructs its future rather than inheriting it.
"Construction frames reached into the void like fingers grasping toward forever, still building, still becoming."

## The Choice Made
Sera doesn't look back. Whatever she was before tonight — the woman who laughed, who reached, who straightened his collar without thinking — that woman made a choice. This woman will live with it.
> For as long as living means.
"This woman would live with it. For as long as living meant."
