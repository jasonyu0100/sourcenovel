# Chapter 3 Route: The Fracture

Idris registers the knock before it happens — a resonance signature approaching their door, familiar but running hot. Sera is still beside him on the couch, datapad dark, her breathing slow. The evening they've been protecting is about to end.

The door opens to Maren Caul — a fellow Adept from their unit, someone they've deployed with a dozen times. Idris reads her before she speaks: elevated pulse, Discipline partially manifested, the particular tension of someone who has already made a choice and is terrified of it. She doesn't apologize for the hour.

Maren delivers the news in clipped fragments — the government has formally split, the Senate is dissolved, military assets are being recalled by origin. Not tomorrow. Now. Transports are already staging. Idris notices how Maren positions herself in the doorway, as if the threshold matters, as if coming fully inside would mean something different.

Sera rises and asks the questions Idris doesn't want to. Which units have moved. Which officers have been detained. Whether the Ascendancy intake station is accepting voluntary transfers or only recalling by birth registry. Maren's answers come fast and anxious — she's Sovereignty-born, like Sera. She's here to say goodbye.

Idris watches Maren's heartbeat spike when she mentions Torren — he's already on a transport, didn't even clear out his quarters. More names follow: Vasquez, Chen, Orin-Daal. People they served with. People who chose. The pattern Sera noticed has become a flood, and it's pouring through their doorway.

Maren turns to Idris directly and asks what he's going to do. Her voice carries something like pleading — she wants him to say he's coming with them, wants the most dangerous Adept in their unit on the side she's chosen. Idris feels Sera go still beside him. He feels his own Discipline cataloguing the room like it always does — the distance to every exit, the resonance of every person present, the exact position of the insignia at his collar.

Idris tells Maren he hasn't decided. The words sound hollow even to him. Maren's expression shifts — she expected this, or feared it. She looks at Sera, then back at Idris, and something passes between the two women that Idris can read but not fully interpret. A question about him. An answer about time.

Maren leaves without asking again. The door closes. The silence that follows is different from the silence before — it has weight now, edges. Sera stands at the window, her back to him, looking out at the convoys Idris refused to see earlier. Her posture is still, deliberate, the way she holds herself when she's calculating something she doesn't want to calculate.

Idris reaches for the datapad he's been ignoring all night — the one with his recall orders, the one he told Sera he hadn't opened. He still doesn't open it. He sets it on the table between them instead, like evidence, like a confession. Sera doesn't turn around, but he knows she saw.

The room is the same room it was an hour ago — his books, her jacket, the warm amber lighting. But it doesn't feel like theirs anymore. It feels like a space they're both standing in temporarily, waiting to see who walks out first. Idris realizes he's measuring the distance between them without meaning to. Three meters. A little more.
