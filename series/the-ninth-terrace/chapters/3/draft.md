# the fracture

idris felt her before the knock came.

A resonance signature approaching their door — familiar, but running hot. Maren Caul. He'd deployed with her a dozen times, knew her Discipline's particular frequency the way he knew Sera's breathing. Tonight that frequency was spiked with something that tasted like fear.

Sera was still beside him on the couch, her datapad dark, her head against his shoulder. The evening they'd been protecting — the one where they didn't talk about Torren or the convoys or the word _origin_ — was about to end.

The knock came.

Sera stirred. Idris was already cataloguing: Maren's heartbeat at ninety-two, her Discipline partially manifested without her realizing it, the particular tension in her shoulders that said she'd already made a choice and was terrified of what came next.

He opened the door.

Maren stood in the corridor in field dress, no rank insignia visible. That was the first wrong thing. The second was that she didn't apologize for the hour, didn't make the small social sounds people made when they showed up uninvited. She just stood there, positioned carefully on the threshold, as if coming fully inside would mean something different.

"It's done," she said. "The Senate dissolved an hour ago. Both Thrones are recalling assets by origin."

Sera rose from the couch. Idris felt her attention sharpen the way it always did when information became actionable.

"Which units have moved?" Sera asked.

"Third Fleet declared for the Ascendancy twenty minutes ago. Seventh is holding, but their command staff is already fracturing." Maren's voice was clipped, efficient. The voice of someone delivering a briefing they'd rather not give. "The Sovereignty intake station is processing voluntary transfers, but the Ascendancy is going by birth registry. If you were born in their space, you're being recalled. Not requested. Recalled."

Idris watched Sera process this. Watched her calculate the implications the way she calculated everything — distances, angles, the weight of a decision before it was made. She was Sovereignty-born. Her commission had always been technically irregular, a product of the unified command structure that was currently being dismantled. Maren was the same.

Idris was not.

"Torren?" he asked.

Maren's heartbeat spiked. "Already on a transport. Didn't clear out his quarters. Vasquez and Chen went with him. Orin-Daal is—" She stopped, shook her head. "Orin-Daal was detained two hours ago. Wrong birth certificate, apparently."

The pattern Sera had noticed — the one Idris had dismissed with _people make choices_ — had become a flood. And it was pouring through their doorway.

"What about you?" Sera asked.

"I'm leaving tonight. There's a transport staging at the secondary port — Sovereignty-bound, no manifest filing. Room for more." Maren's eyes moved to Idris. Stayed there. "Both of you, if you want."

The room felt smaller. The amber lighting that had been warm an hour ago now felt like exposure.

"I haven't decided," Idris said.

The words came out flat. Even he could hear how hollow they sounded — the same deflection he'd used about Torren, about the convoys, about everything he didn't want to look at. _People make choices._ Now someone was standing in front of him who had made one, and he was still pretending he could wait.

Maren's expression shifted — disappointment layered over understanding, or maybe the other way around. Her gaze moved to Sera, then back to Idris, and something passed between the two women that he could read but not fully interpret. A question about him. An answer about time. He caught Maren's heartbeat dropping, settling into something like resignation. She'd hoped. She'd known better than to hope.

"The transport leaves at 0400," Maren said. "After that, the window closes."

She didn't ask again. She just stood there for a moment longer, looking at the quarters she'd visited a hundred times — the books stacked on Sera's desk, the jacket draped over Idris's chair, the life they'd built that was about to stop existing — and then she was gone.

The door closed.

Idris stood there for a moment, his hand still on the handle. The metal was cool under his palm. On the other side, he could feel Maren's signature receding down the corridor — faster now, less controlled. She was running. Everyone was running, in one direction or another.

The silence that followed was different from the silence before. It had weight now. Edges. The hum of the climate system seemed louder than it had been. Somewhere in the building, a notification tone chimed — priority military, the distinctive double-pulse that meant someone else's life was about to change.

Sera walked to the window. Outside, the convoys Idris had refused to see earlier were moving in tight formation, running lights blinking in the dark. She didn't say anything. Her posture was still, deliberate — the way she held herself when she was calculating something she didn't want to calculate.

Idris reached for the datapad he'd been ignoring all night. The one with his recall orders. The one he'd told Sera he hadn't opened. He still didn't open it. He set it on the table between them instead, face down, like evidence. Like a confession.

Sera didn't turn around. But he knew she saw.

The room was the same room it had been an hour ago. His books. Her jacket. The warm amber lighting that was starting to feel like a lie. But it didn't feel like theirs anymore. It felt like a space they were both standing in temporarily, waiting to see who would move first.

Idris found himself measuring the distance between them. Three meters. A little more.

An hour ago, that distance had been nothing. Now it felt like the beginning of something much larger.

"She wanted you to say yes," Sera said quietly, still facing the window. "She wanted you to come with us."

_Us._

The word landed harder than anything Maren had said.

"I know," Idris said.

Sera's reflection in the glass was motionless. "Do you?"

He didn't answer. He didn't have an answer. All he had was the datapad on the table, face down, and the space between them that was growing even though neither of them had moved.

Outside, another convoy passed. The running lights traced lines across the dark, moving toward destinations Idris didn't want to name.

The room stayed silent. But it wasn't the same silence they'd shared before Maren knocked. That silence had been warm, deliberate, a choice they'd made together. This silence was the space where a conversation should be — the conversation neither of them knew how to start.

Sera's hand moved to the window frame. Her fingers pressed flat against the glass, anchoring herself. Idris recognized the gesture. He'd seen her do it a hundred times before difficult briefings, before impossible missions, before moments that required her to be something other than what she felt.

He wanted to cross the room. He wanted to stand behind her and put his hand over hers the way he had an hour ago, when the evening was still theirs. But three meters felt like a boundary now, and he wasn't sure which side of it he belonged on.

The datapad sat on the table between them. Face down. Unopened.

They both knew what it said.
